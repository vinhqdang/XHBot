\section{Experiments and Comprehensive Analysis}

To empirically validate the superiority of XHBot against relation camouflage, we conducted a rigorous suite of experiments across three distinct, large-scale bot detection benchmarks.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Datasets}
We evaluate our framework on three widely adopted benchmarks that represent distinct eras and scales of bot evolution:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TwiBot-20}~\cite{feng2021twibot20}: A comprehensive graph-based benchmark containing 229,573 users and 455,958 follow relationships. With a bot ratio of roughly 29\%, it serves as the primary standard for modern relational GNN comparison.
    \item \textbf{TwiBot-22}~\cite{feng2022twibot22}: The largest heterogeneous information network benchmark available, scaling to $\sim$1,000,000 users and over 170 million relationships across four node types and six edge types. This dataset explicitly tests the scalability and multi-relational capacity of XHBot.
    \item \textbf{Cresci-2017}~\cite{cresci2017paradigm}: A legacy dataset featuring distinct families of social spambots (e.g., traditional vs. third-generation). It provides a high-density, high-imbalance environment ($\sim$68\% bots) to validate heterophily resilience against specific, known bot-ring topologies.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We utilize a robust suite of metrics to evaluate both detection performance and forensic transparency.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bot Detection Performance:} We report F1 Score, Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC), Accuracy, and the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). Following established protocols, \textbf{F1 Score} serves as our primary comparative metric due to severe class imbalance.
    \item \textbf{Explanation Quality Metrics:} To validate the Hierarchical Forensic Explanation (HFE) module, we evaluate \textbf{Fidelity+} (accuracy drop when the explanation subgraph is removed), \textbf{Fidelity-} (accuracy when only the subgraph is retained), and \textbf{Sparsity} (conciseness of the explanation).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Settings and Computational Efficiency}
Models were implemented using PyTorch Geometric and trained on an NVIDIA A100 GPU. Unless otherwise noted, the GNN backbone uses $L = 2$ THCA layers with a hidden channel dimension of $d_h = 64$ and a dropout rate of $0.5$ applied between layers. The CPD module projects each node representation into two $d' = 32$-dimensional sub-spaces (behavioural and social-positioning codes), and uses an InfoNCE temperature $\tau_{nce} = 0.1$. The total training objective combines the classification cross-entropy with the disentanglement and contrastive terms, $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda_{MI}\,\mathcal{L}_{MI} + \lambda_{NCE}\,\mathcal{L}_{NCE}$, with $\lambda_{MI} = 0.1$ and $\lambda_{NCE} = 0.5$; the SGTR pruning threshold is set to $\tau = 0.5$. We optimised using Adam (learning rate $0.01$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) for $200$ epochs with early stopping on the validation F1. Regarding computational efficiency, XHBot maintains a competitive footprint: training requires approximately $4.2$ seconds per epoch on TwiBot-20, with sub-millisecond per-node inference latency, supporting feasibility for real-world deployment.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:comprehensive_results} and Figure \ref{fig:comparison}, XHBot establishes a new State-of-the-Art performance hierarchy across all three benchmarks. 

\textbf{The Winner (XHBot):} Our proposed framework comprehensively dominates the baseline models. On the highly dense TwiBot-20 dataset, XHBot achieves an F1 score of 0.9474, outperforming the closest baseline by +9.64\%. This massive margin extends to the million-node TwiBot-22 graph, where XHBot (0.9125 F1) outperforms the runner-up by +13.15\% F1. The fundamental reason for this superiority is the Tri-Channel Heterophily-Aware Aggregation (THCA) coupled with Contrastive Prototype Disentanglement (CPD). While standard GNNs mathematically average bot anomalies with their human neighbors during message passing, THCA explicitly routes heterophilic camouflage edges into a separate max-pooled channel, thereby preserving the anomaly signal irrespective of graph density.

\textbf{The Runner-Up (HW-GNN):} HW-GNN~\cite{liu2025hwgnn} emerges as the second-best model (0.8510 F1 on TwiBot-20). HW-GNN's performance is commendable because it employs a homophily-aware spectral network that attempts to filter frequencies associated with camouflaged clusters. However, it falls significantly short of XHBot because its Gaussian-window filtering is symmetric and uniform; it lacks a dedicated self-identity preservation channel. Consequently, under extreme heterophily (like in TwiBot-22), HW-GNN still suffers from over-smoothing, as it cannot fully disentangle intrinsic behavioral traits from surrounding neighborhood noise without a contrastive objective.

\textbf{The Competitive Middle-Tier (NeighborSense \& CARE-GNN):} NeighborSense~\cite{li2026neighborsense} and CARE-GNN~\cite{dou2020caregnn} exhibit moderate resilience (0.8245 F1 and 0.7930 F1 on TwiBot-20, respectively). NeighborSense attempts to dynamically perceive neighborhood structures, while CARE-GNN employs reinforcement learning for neighbor filtering. However, both frameworks are optimized primarily for static or slightly noisy fraud graphs. They lack the Spectral-Guided Topology Refinement (SGTR) required to actively prune the massively coordinated, adversarial follow-relationships prevalent in modern social botnets, leaving them susceptible to relation camouflage.

\textbf{The Baseline Failures (BotRGCN \& RGT):} Early architectures like BotRGCN~\cite{feng2021botrgcn} and RGT~\cite{feng2022rgt} sit firmly at the bottom of the hierarchy, struggling significantly particularly on TwiBot-22 (F1 $\approx$ 0.68 -- 0.70). These models utilize relational graph convolutions or multi-relation transformer attentions that intrinsically assume homophily. When camouflaged bots strategically dedicate up to 90\% of their outbound edges to genuine human accounts, these baseline aggregators suffer from severe representation dilution. Their mathematically uniform message passing effectively weaponizes the bots' camouflage against the detector itself, underscoring the absolute necessity for the heterophily-aware architecture introduced by XHBot.

\subsection{Explainability Evaluation}

Given that forensic transparency is a primary objective of XHBot, we rigorously evaluate the Hierarchical Forensic Explanation (HFE) module against standard graph explainer baselines. We compare our integrated HFE against GNNExplainer~\cite{ying2019gnnexplainer} and PGExplainer~\cite{luo2020pgexplainer} applied directly over the RGT baseline model.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:explainability}, the HFE module significantly outperforms post-hoc explainers applied to standard baselines. The high \textbf{Fidelity+} (0.415) indicates that when HFE's identified forensic subgraph is masked, the model's confidence in predicting a ``bot'' drops drastically, proving the subgraph contains the true causal anomaly. Conversely, the exceptionally low \textbf{Fidelity-} (0.112) demonstrates that the isolated subgraph alone is highly sufficient to maintain the correct prediction. Furthermore, HFE achieves an impressive \textbf{Sparsity} score of 0.895, meaning it successfully prunes away nearly 90\% of irrelevant neighborhood noise edges to return a highly concise, human-readable rationale to the platform moderator. 

Qualitatively, HFE's Level-2 (Community) and Level-3 (Motif) outputs consistently surfaced dense ``star-formations'' (a single bot rapidly following hundreds of disconnected humans) within the TwiBot-22 graph. This explicitly confirms that XHBot is detecting the exact topological fingerprint of heterophilic camouflage strategies, fulfilling the ``eXplainable'' promise of its architecture.
